Movie Revenue Analytics: Exploring the Relationship Between Ratings and Box Office Performance

Analyzing whether positive reviews influence audience interest using box office data (2000–2024)

Project Overview

This project investigates whether positive reviews influence audience interest and financial success in the movie industry. Using box office data from 2000–2024, the analysis explores the relationship between movie ratings and worldwide revenue through descriptive analytics, inferential statistics and predictive modelling.

Problem Statement

This project investigates whether the statement "Positive Reviews Influence Audience Interest" holds true by analyzing the relationship between audience ratings and worldwide box office revenue.

The study examines whether higher-rated movies demonstrate stronger box office performance using descriptive analytics, inferential statistics and predictive modelling.

Hypothesis Statement

The study evaluates whether movies with higher audience ratings demonstrate better worldwide box office performance.

Null Hypothesis (H₀)

There is no statistically significant relationship between audience ratings and worldwide box office revenue.

Alternative Hypothesis (H₁)

There is a statistically significant relationship between audience ratings and worldwide box office revenue, where higher-rated movies tend to achieve higher worldwide revenue.

Dataset

  • Kaggle Box Office Dataset (2000–2024)
    • View Dataset on Kaggle
  • Approximately 5,000 movie records
  • Features included: Movie title | release year | genre | rating, vote count | worldwide revenue | domestic revenue | foreign revenue | production country | language

Technologies

  • Programming Language: R
  • Libraries: ggplot2 | dplyr | dunn.test
  • Development Environment: RStudio
  • Statistical Techniques: Exploratory Data Analysis | Shapiro-Wilk Test | Kruskal-Wallis Test | Dunn's Post Hoc Test | Spearman Correlation | Linear Regression

Analytical Approach

This project follows a structured data analytics workflow covering data preprocessing, descriptive analysis, statistical testing, and predictive modelling to investigate the relationship between audience ratings and worldwide box office revenue.

Data Preprocessing

Prepared raw movie data for statistical analysis and predictive modelling by improving data quality and creating analysis-ready features.

  • Handled missing values and inconsistent records.
  • Converted revenue and rating fields into suitable numeric formats.
  • Created derived features such as movie era and Hit/Flop categories.
  • Prepared cleaned dataset for statistical testing and modelling.

Descriptive Analytics

Analyzed movie characteristics and revenue patterns to understand trends between audience ratings and box office performance.

  • Revenue distribution analysis.
  • Rating distribution analysis.
  • Comparison of revenue across rating categories.
  • Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) using visualizations.

Inferential Analytics

Applied statistical hypothesis testing to evaluate whether differences and relationships exist between audience ratings and worldwide revenue.

  • Shapiro-Wilk normality test.
  • Kruskal-Wallis test for comparing rating groups.
  • Dunn's Post Hoc analysis for identifying significant group differences.
  • Spearman correlation analysis to measure the relationship between ratings and revenue.

Predictive Analytics

Developed a regression model to evaluate the ability of audience ratings to predict worldwide box office revenue.

  • Built a Simple Linear Regression model using ratings as the predictor variable.
  • Performed train-test split for model evaluation.
  • Evaluated model performance using R² and RMSE metrics.

Key Findings

  • Movies with higher audience ratings generally showed higher worldwide revenue trends; however, the relationship was relatively weak.
  • Kruskal-Wallis and Dunn's Post Hoc tests identified statistically significant differences between movie rating groups, (p < 0.001), leading to the rejection of the null hypothesis and indicating that audience ratings are associated with differences in worldwide revenue.
  • Spearman correlation showed a positive but weak relationship between audience ratings and worldwide revenue (ρ = 0.14), indicating that higher ratings alone do not strongly determine box office success.
  • The linear regression model achieved an R² value of 0.034, meaning audience ratings explained only approximately 3.4% of revenue variation. This highlights that other factors such as marketing investment, production budget, franchise popularity, cast influence, and audience reach may play a larger role in determining box office performance.

Limitations & Future Improvements

While audience ratings show a positive relationship with worldwide box office revenue, this study has several limitations that affect predictive accuracy.

  • Limited Predictive Variables: Audience ratings alone cannot accurately predict box office revenue, as commercial success depends on multiple factors beyond audience reception.
  • Additional Features: Future models could incorporate variables such as production budget, marketing expenditure, cast popularity, director influence, genre, franchise information, release timing, and audience engagement metrics.
  • Data Improvements: Future studies could consider inflation-adjusted revenue, regional market differences, and additional movie performance indicators.
  • Advanced Modelling: Machine learning approaches such as Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, XGBoost, or other ensemble methods could be explored to improve revenue prediction accuracy.
